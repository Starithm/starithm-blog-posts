## Opening

Gravitational waves have opened a new window on the universe's most violent events, but our understanding of how these ripples in spacetime interact with matter remains incomplete. A new study by Kakkat, Beckering Vinckers, Bishop, and colleagues challenges the long-held assumption that gravitational wave energy dissipation in viscous matter is negligible. By examining how gravitational waves propagate through realistic astrophysical environments—including the curved spacetime around black holes—the researchers find that under certain conditions, the damping effect can become *substantial enough to significantly attenuate waves or even contribute to observable transients like gamma-ray bursts*.

## What they found

The authors extend their previous work on linearized perturbations in flat spacetime to more realistic scenarios involving curved backgrounds. Their key finding is that gravitational wave damping and the associated heating of viscous fluids are *enhanced* when the source and matter are separated by distances smaller than the gravitational wave wavelength—a regime that becomes increasingly relevant in strong-field environments.

The team investigated three astrophysical contexts where these effects may be important: core-collapse supernovae, binary neutron star mergers, and accretion flows around binary black hole mergers. In each case, the enhanced interaction between gravitational waves and viscous matter could lead to observable consequences. Most notably, the authors suggest that this mechanism could potentially contribute to gamma-ray burst production, though they frame this as a scenario *where these effects may be relevant* rather than a definitive explanation.

The analysis moves beyond the idealized Minkowski background of their earlier work to examine Schwarzschild spacetime and static spherically symmetric configurations. This progression toward more realistic geometries is crucial: the curved spacetime near compact objects amplifies the coupling between gravitational waves and matter, making previously negligible effects potentially astrophysically significant.

## Why it matters

This work bridges an important gap in multi-messenger astronomy. While electromagnetic and neutrino observations have become standard companions to gravitational wave detections, the direct coupling between gravitational waves and matter has received less attention. If gravitational wave damping can be substantial in certain environments, it could affect both the interpretation of observed gravitational wave signals and the production of electromagnetic counterparts—particularly in scenarios involving accretion or dense fluid environments.

The potential connection to gamma-ray bursts is particularly intriguing, as it suggests gravitational wave energy dissipation might contribute to the energetics of these extreme transients, adding another layer to their already complex physics.

## What's next

The authors identify several open questions. Detailed modeling of specific astrophysical scenarios—particularly the structure and viscosity profiles of accretion disks and merger ejecta—will be essential to determine whether gravitational wave damping reaches observable levels in real events. Additionally, distinguishing this mechanism's contribution from other energy sources in phenomena like gamma-ray bursts will require careful comparative analysis.
